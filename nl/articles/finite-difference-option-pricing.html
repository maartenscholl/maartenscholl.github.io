The Equation on a Grid

The value V(S,t)V(S,t)V(S,t) of a European option under Black-Scholes obeys

∂V∂t+rS∂V∂S+12σ2S2∂2V∂S2=rV. \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} = rV. ∂t∂V​+rS∂S∂V​+21​σ2S2∂S2∂2V​=rV.

Monte Carlo integrates the terminal payoff over simulated paths; the COS method integrates it against the characteristic function. The finite-difference approach does something more direct: it discretises the equation itself onto a grid in price and time and marches the known payoff at expiry backwards to today. The payoff is the terminal condition, so it is natural to run time in reverse. Write

τ=T−t, \tau = T - t, τ=T−t,

so the payoff sits at τ=0\tau = 0τ=0 and we integrate forward in τ\tauτ. With the usual Greek notation Θ=∂V/∂t\Theta = \partial V/\partial tΘ=∂V/∂t, Δ=∂V/∂S\Delta = \partial V/\partial SΔ=∂V/∂S, Γ=∂2V/∂S2\Gamma = \partial^2 V/\partial S^2Γ=∂2V/∂S2, the equation reads Θ+rSΔ+12σ2S2Γ=rV\Theta + rS\Delta + \tfrac12\sigma^2 S^2\Gamma = rVΘ+rSΔ+21​σ2S2Γ=rV. Two Grids, and a Trap

Before any discretisation there is a choice of grid, and it is the one place in this procedure that is easiest to get subtly wrong. There are two standard options.

The untransformed grid places nodes at Si=i δSS_i = i\,\delta SSi​=iδS, evenly spaced in price. The PDE keeps its SSS-dependent coefficients rSrSrS and 12σ2S2\tfrac12\sigma^2 S^221​σ2S2.

The log grid changes variables to x=ln⁡Sx = \ln Sx=lnS. Substituting x=ln⁡Sx = \ln Sx=lnS and τ=T−t\tau = T-tτ=T−t into the equation and writing U(x,τ)=V(S,t)U(x,\tau) = V(S,t)U(x,τ)=V(S,t) gives a constant-coefficient equation,

∂U∂τ=12σ2∂2U∂x2+(r−12σ2)∂U∂x−rU. \frac{\partial U}{\partial \tau} = \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial x^2} + \left(r - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} - rU. ∂τ∂U​=21​σ2∂x2∂2U​+(r−21​σ2)∂x∂U​−rU.

The transformation earns its keep: the diffusion and drift coefficients no longer depend on SSS. Equal steps in xxx are geometric steps in SSS, so the grid is fine where SSS is small and a distant upper boundary costs only a handful of extra nodes.

The coefficients belong to the grid: rSrSrS and 12σ2S2\tfrac12\sigma^2 S^221​σ2S2 on the SSS grid, the constants (r−12σ2)(r - \tfrac12\sigma^2)(r−21​σ2) and 12σ2\tfrac12\sigma^221​σ2 on the log grid. Reaching for the wrong pair is an easy mistake and a surprisingly costly one. The scheme below is built on the SSS grid; the section that follows shows what happens if you borrow the log-grid constants for it.

FTCS: the Explicit Scheme

Forward-Time Centred-Space substitutes the three stencils into the PDE and solves for the single unknown Vin+1V^{n+1}_iVin+1​ in terms of three known values at the previous step. On the untransformed SSS grid, because Si=i δSS_i = i\,\delta SSi​=iδS the factor δS\delta SδS cancels against the SSS and S2S^2S2 in the coefficients, and everything collapses to functions of the index iii alone:

Each new value is read off directly, with no system to solve.

Stability

The explicit scheme is only conditionally stable. Its coefficients must stay well behaved, and the binding requirement is bi≥0b_i \ge 0bi​≥0, worst at the top of the grid where iii is largest:

δt≤1σ2imax⁡2+r≈1σ2N2. \delta t \le \frac{1}{\sigma^2 i_{\max}^2 + r} \approx \frac{1}{\sigma^2 N^2}. δt≤σ2imax2​+r1​≈σ2N21​.

The timestep must shrink like 1/N21/N^21/N2. Doubling the asset resolution quadruples the number of timesteps: N=20N = 20N=20 needs 404040 steps, N=100N = 100N=100 needs 100010001000, N=200N = 200N=200 needs 400040004000. The work grows like N3N^3N3, which is the standing weakness of the explicit method and the reason to pay for an implicit one.

Crank-Nicolson: the Implicit Scheme

Crank-Nicolson averages the spatial operator over the old and new time levels. Writing the discretised right-hand side of the PDE as a tridiagonal operator L\mathcal{L}L acting on the value vector, the update is

(I−12δt L)Vn+1=(I+12δt L)Vn. \left(I - \tfrac12\delta t\,\mathcal{L}\right)V^{n+1} = \left(I + \tfrac12\delta t\,\mathcal{L}\right)V^n. (I−21​δtL)Vn+1=(I+21​δtL)Vn.

Now Vn+1V^{n+1}Vn+1 appears on both sides: the three unknowns at each step are coupled, and each step solves a tridiagonal system. That system is cheap, one LU (or Thomas) sweep in O(N)\mathcal{O}(N)O(N), and buys two things. Crank-Nicolson is unconditionally stable, so the timestep is chosen for accuracy rather than survival, and its error is O(δt2+δS2)\mathcal{O}(\delta t^2 + \delta S^2)O(δt2+δS2) against the explicit scheme’s O(δt+δS2)\mathcal{O}(\delta t + \delta S^2)O(δt+δS2). One implementation note worth stating, since it is easy to miss: factor the matrix once, up front, and reuse the factorisation each step. Re-inverting it on every timestep is O(N3)\mathcal{O}(N^3)O(N3) per step and throws the whole advantage away.

Boundary Conditions

The grid is finite, so the scheme needs values at the edges. For a call:

  • At S=0S = 0S=0 the option is worthless, V(0,τ)=0V(0, \tau) = 0V(0,τ)=0.
  • As S→∞S \to \inftyS→∞ the call behaves like the forward, V(Smax⁡,τ)=Smax⁡−Ke−rτV(S_{\max}, \tau) = S_{\max} - K e^{-r\tau}V(Smax​,τ)=Smax​−Ke−rτ: the stock minus the discounted strike. The strike is discounted, not grown; it is easy to write the intrinsic value Smax⁡−KS_{\max} - KSmax​−K and carry it at the risk-free rate by mistake, which has the discounting backwards. An alternative that avoids committing to a value is the linearity condition Γ=0\Gamma = 0Γ=0, imposed as VN=2VN−1−VN−2V_N = 2V_{N-1} - V_{N-2}VN​=2VN−1​−VN−2​.

Even the correct condition is only asymptotic, and imposing it at a finite Smax⁡S_{\max}Smax​ introduces an error, because at a modest boundary the true value still sits below Smax⁡−Ke−rτS_{\max} - K e^{-r\tau}Smax​−Ke−rτ: the option retains a little time value and its delta is not yet one. Pinning the boundary above the true value biases the whole solution up, and refining the grid does not remove it. A boundary placed generously, several times the strike, keeps the effect negligible.

Results

With a generous domain and either scheme, the finite-difference price converges to the analytical value. Convergence is oscillatory: as the grid is refined, the node nearest the spot alternately straddles it from above and below.

The grid also gives the Greeks for free, as differences of the value surface it already holds. A centred difference of the solution recovers the delta across the whole range of SSS without any extra solving.

Takeaways

  • Finite differences price an option by discretising the Black-Scholes PDE on a grid and marching the payoff backwards, giving the whole value surface and the Greeks in one solve.
  • The spatial grid is a real choice. Equal steps in SSS keep SSS-dependent coefficients; equal steps in ln⁡S\ln SlnS make them constant and reach a far boundary cheaply. The coefficients belong to the grid, and mixing the two is a silent, severe error.
  • The explicit scheme is simple but conditionally stable, with a timestep shrinking like 1/N21/N^21/N2. Crank-Nicolson removes that constraint for the price of a tridiagonal solve per step and a better order of accuracy.
  • Boundary placement matters as much as grid resolution: too tight a domain caps the accuracy no matter how fine the mesh.
