Characteristic Function Approach

Under geometric Brownian motion, the log-price increment ln⁡(ST/K)\ln(S_T/K)ln(ST​/K) has a known characteristic function. Rather than working with the probability density directly, we exploit the fact that any density f(x)f(x)f(x) on a finite interval [a,b][a, b][a,b] admits a Fourier cosine expansion

where the prime denotes that the first term is halved, and

Ak=∫abf(x)cos⁡ ⁣(kπ(x−a)b−a)dx. A_k = \int_a^b f(x) \cos\!\left(\frac{k\pi(x - a)}{b - a}\right) dx. Ak​=∫ab​f(x)cos(b−akπ(x−a)​)dx.

The key insight is that AkA_kAk​ can be recovered from the characteristic function ϕ(u)=E[eiux]\phi(u) = \mathbb{E}[e^{iux}]ϕ(u)=E[eiux] without knowing fff explicitly:

Ak≈Re⁡ ⁣[ϕ ⁣(kπb−a)e−ikπab−a]. A_k \approx \operatorname{Re}\!\left[\phi\!\left(\frac{k\pi}{b - a}\right) e^{-i\frac{k\pi a}{b - a}}\right]. Ak​≈Re[ϕ(b−akπ​)e−ib−akπa​].

For the GBM model, the characteristic function of x=ln⁡(ST/K)x = \ln(S_T/K)x=ln(ST​/K) is

The cosine expansion converges rapidly for smooth densities. With only a handful of terms, the standard normal density is already well approximated.

Chi and Psi Coefficients

The option price requires computing the inner product of the density coefficients FkF_kFk​ with the payoff coefficients VkV_kVk​. The payoff coefficients decompose into two integrals, χk\chi_kχk​ and ψk\psi_kψk​, that can be evaluated in closed form.

Truncation Range

The cosine expansion requires a finite domain [a,b][a, b][a,b]. This range must be wide enough to capture the mass of the density, but not so wide that the cosine basis functions oscillate excessively relative to the number of terms NNN.

Two formulations appear in the literature. The first centres the range on the forward:

a,b=ln⁡(S0/K)+rτ∓Lσ2τ, a, b = \ln(S_0/K) + r\tau \mp L\sqrt{\sigma^2 \tau}, a,b=ln(S0​/K)+rτ∓Lσ2τ​,

and the second uses the first cumulant (the mean of the log-price increment):

a,b=ln⁡(S0/K)+(r−12σ2)τ∓Lσ2τ, a, b = \ln(S_0/K) + \left(r - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)\tau \mp L\sqrt{\sigma^2 \tau}, a,b=ln(S0​/K)+(r−21​σ2)τ∓Lσ2τ​,

where LLL controls the number of standard deviations included. Both formulations converge, but the cumulant-based formula places the interval symmetrically around the true mean of the distribution, which leads to faster convergence at moderate NNN.

The COS Pricing Formula

Combining the density and payoff coefficients, the discounted option price is

V=e−rτ∑k=0N−1′Fk⋅Vk, V = e^{-r\tau} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{}' F_k \cdot V_k, V=e−rτk=0∑N−1​′Fk​⋅Vk​,

where

Fk=Re⁡ ⁣[ϕ ⁣(kπb−a)eikπb−a(ln⁡(S0/K)−a)] F_k = \operatorname{Re}\!\left[\phi\!\left(\frac{k\pi}{b - a}\right) e^{i\frac{k\pi}{b - a}(\ln(S_0/K) - a)}\right] Fk​=Re[ϕ(b−akπ​)eib−akπ​(ln(S0​/K)−a)]

and

Vk=2b−a K (χk−ψk)(call),Vk=2b−a K (ψk−χk)(put). V_k = \frac{2}{b - a}\, K\, (\chi_k - \psi_k) \quad \text{(call)}, \qquad V_k = \frac{2}{b - a}\, K\, (\psi_k - \chi_k) \quad \text{(put)}. Vk​=b−a2​K(χk​−ψk​)(call),Vk​=b−a2​K(ψk​−χk​)(put).

The computational cost is O(N)\mathcal{O}(N)O(N) per option price: all NNN terms involve elementary trigonometric and exponential evaluations with no matrix inversions or root-finding. This makes the COS method substantially faster than PDE or Monte Carlo methods for European-style contracts.
